Injera

Cultural Description

Injera is the foundation of Ethiopian and Eritrean cuisine—a spongy, sourdough flatbread that serves as both plate and utensil. Made from teff flour, this unique bread has been a staple for thousands of years in the Horn of Africa.

The slightly tangy flavor comes from a natural fermentation process, and its spongy texture is perfect for soaking up the rich, flavorful stews (wats) that are traditionally served on top. Eating injera is a communal experience, often shared from a large platter with family and friends.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups teff flour (or substitute with 1 cup teff + 1 cup all-purpose flour)
  • 3 cups water
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 teaspoon baking powder (optional, for quicker rise)
  • Oil for greasing the pan

Cooking Instructions

Click to expand step-by-step instructions
  1. In a large bowl, mix teff flour with water until smooth and well combined.
  2. Cover the bowl with a clean cloth and let it ferment at room temperature for 24-72 hours. The longer it ferments, the more sour it becomes.
  3. After fermentation, the batter should be bubbly and have a slightly sour smell.
  4. Pour off any water that has accumulated on top and add salt. Mix well.
  5. If the batter is too thick, add a little water to achieve a consistency similar to pancake batter.
  6. Heat a non-stick pan or traditional clay pan (mitad) over medium heat and lightly grease it.
  7. Pour about 1/2 cup of batter into the center of the pan and quickly tilt in a circular motion to spread it evenly.
  8. Cover the pan and cook for 2-3 minutes until holes form on the surface and the edges begin to lift.
  9. Do not flip the injera—it cooks from one side only.
  10. Remove from pan and place on a clean cloth. Repeat with remaining batter.
  11. Serve with traditional Ethiopian stews like doro wat or misir wat.
